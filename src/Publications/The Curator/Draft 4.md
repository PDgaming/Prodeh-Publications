# Chapter 2

Days, or maybe weeks, have passed. Time is meaningless in this room with no sunlight. There is no day or night, only periods of wakefulness and drugged sleep. They are fed a tasteless, nutrient paste through a tube at night, administered by the man when they are unconscious.

The signs of fatigue are carved into all of them. The young boy's eyes are permanently red-rimmed. The policeman, Mark, has lost weight; his uniform now hangs loosely on his frame. David is gone. His chair is empty, cleaned so thoroughly it looks like it was never occupied.

The man's focus has shifted to Mark this time.

"A protector," He says, circling Mark's chair. "Your identity is intrinsically tied to your physical capability. To your hands. Your feet. Let us explore the source of that identity."

He goes to the tables and picks up a pair of heavy bolt cutters.

Mark doesn't beg. He spits. "You'll get nothing from me, you sick fuck."

"I require nothing from you," the man said as he positioned the cutters. "I require only your body. It will tell me everything."

The crunch was sickeningly loud in the enclosed space. Marked screamed, a sound of pure and unadulterated agony.

The man always works methodically. First the feet, then the hands. Each time, he places the severed part into a labeled plastic baggie, which he meticulously stores in a small refrigerator in his workshop. Mark, the protector, is now just a torso and a head. His eyes are dull and vacant.

During this time, the man's torment of the young boy, Leo, escalated too. But it wasn't physical. He put headphones on the boy, forcing him to listen to distorted, looping audio: a woman crying, a man laughing, a baby screaming. He shows him images on a screen, things too quick to process but horrifying all the same. Occasionally, he would walk over and slap Leo across the face, not too hard, but just enough to keep him in a state of constant, shivering terror.

She notices it all, observing the "ritual" the man performs. The man enters, undresses, and puts on the apron. He works, redresses, and leaves. That is the only clock she has. She feels drugs coursing through her, a warm fog that dulls her thoughts. She fights it, focusing on the details. The way the man's hands hesitate before he makes a cut. The flicker of what might be disappointment when someone passes out too soon.

# Chapter 3

They are all asleep, drugged. The man moves silently among them, a ghost in his normal clothes. He checks their IV drips, ensuring the dose is correct.

He stops at Ana's chair. He reaches for the tube to change the bag. His fingers brush against the port.

He stops.

He leans closer. The connection is loose. The drip line has been carefully disconnected from the port. A tiny bubble of air is trapped in the line.

His eyes move to Ana's face. She is asleep, her breathing even.

He stares at her for a long, long moment. He doesn't reconnect the line. He simply moves on to the next chair, his routine uninterrupted.

# Chapter 4

The ritual begins again. The door opened. The man entered. The undressing. The apron.

But today is different. Her heart hammers in her chest, a frantic drum against the chemical calm. She is clear-headed for the first time. The fog is gone.

The man walks directly to her chair. He says nothing, and works on the mechanism on her left hand. With a series of precise clicks, it unlocks. He swings it open. Her hand, pale and stiff, is free. She wanted to punch him. But not yet.

He does the same to her right hand. Then moves down to her leg cuffs.

She waits patiently for him to open both her legs. And with the final click, her legs are free too.

This is it.

She draws her legs up, her muscles screaming in protest after weeks of atrophy, and kicks him with all her might.

The man grunts, stumbling, crashing into his tool table. Instruments clatter to the floor.

She is out of her, unsteady, but the pure adrenaline is fuel enough. She grabs the heaviest thing she can find: a heavy steel mallet. The cold weight of it in her hand is the most real thing she has felt since waking up.

The man pushes himself up, turning to face her. There is no fear in his eyes. No surprise. He doesn't raise his hands to defend himself. He doesn't speak.

She lunges, swinging the mallet. The first blow catches him in the shoulder. He grunts, stumbling back, but his expression doesn't change. She swings again, a wild, clumsy arc, and it connects with his ribs. A sickening crack. He falls to his knees, his breath coming in ragged gasps, but he still just watches her.

She stands over him, the mallet raised. She could end it. She could crush his skull. But she sees it in his eyes, that is what he wants. Not the pain, but the data. The reaction. The entropy.

She drops the mallet. It clatters to the floor, the sound loud and final.

Her eyes dart around the room. They land on Arthur, who has watched the entire exchange with a placid, unwavering gaze. A new, colder idea forms in her mind, sharp and clear as the drug-free air in her lungs.

She walks over to the crude table and picks up the handsaw. Its teeth are rusty and menacing. She doesn't look at the man. She walks toward Arthur.

Arthur didn't flinch. He doesn't cry out. He just watches her approach, his eyes filled with unwavering loyalty.

She presses the serrated edge of the saw against Arthur's throat. The old man's skin is paper-thin. She can feel his pulse, a slow and steady rhythm against the steel.

"Open the door," she says, her voice a low growl. She doesn't look at the man. She keeps her eyes locked on Arthur's.

For the first time, a change crosses the man's face. It's not fear. It's... disappointment. Not in her, but in himself. He miscalculated. He assumed the variable was her will to live. He didn't account for her will to manipulate. He was so focused on the pieces, he forgot one was a detective.

Slowly, painfully, the man gets to his feet. He limps toward the door, his body a broken map of her rage. He places his hand on the biometric pad.

The door clicks open with a heavy thunk.

She doesn't hesitate. She pulls the saw away from Arthur's throat and runs. She doesn't look back. She scrambles up the concrete stairs, away from the warmth, the smell, the silence, and into the unknown.

# Chapter 5

The door at the top is plain wood. She bursts through it and collapses onto the floor of a normal, carpeted hallway. The air is cool, fresh. Sunlight streams through a window at the far end. It's so bright it hurts her eyes. She can hear birds chirping. The normalcy is almost like a physical assault.

She gets to her feet, her legs shaking. She's in a suburban home. Family photos on the walls. A coat rack. It's perfect. It's mundane. It's horrifying. She knows this house. Not personally, but from a file. A person of interest in a case she was working on, a case that had gone cold. A dead end. He was closer than she ever knew.

She finds the front door, fumbles with the lock, and stumbles out into the blinding sun of a quiet, tree-lined street.

# Chapter 5

A stark, fluorescent-lit room. Ana sits across a table from two other detectives. She's wrapped in a blanket, a mug of coffee in her hands. She looks haunted.

"He's gone," one of the detectives is saying. "The house was cleaned out. By the time we got there, it was sterile. But we found the basement. And Arthur."

"He's in rehabilitation," the other adds. "Physically, he'll recover. Mentally... he keeps asking for his 'Master.' He's convinced Robert will come back to punish him for failing."

Ana just nods, staring into her coffee. She knows Robert isn't coming back for Arthur. Arthur was a successful experiment. The data was collected. He's obsolete.

# Chapter 6

A moving truck is parked in front of a different house, in a different neighborhood, in a different state. Robert, now with a cane and a pronounced limp, is directing movers. His face is a mask of polite frailty. He has a new, slightly darker hair color.

An elderly neighbor lady comes over with a plate of cookies.

"Welcome to the neighborhood, dear!" she says, beaming. "I'm Mildred. It's so lovely to have a new face on the street. You just let me know if you need anything at all."

Robert turns to her, and the transformation is seamless. His eyes crinkle. A warm, grandfatherly smile spreads across his face.

"That's so kind of you, Mildred," he says, his voice gentle and sincere. "I'm Robert. It's a pleasure to be here."

He takes a cookie. As Mildred bustles back to her house, Robert's smile slowly fades. He looks at his new house, a blank canvas. He takes a bite of the cookie. His eyes are distant, thoughtful. He is not planning. He is not remembering. He is simply, quietly, content.

The experiment is over. A new one is about to begin.
