The gravel crunched under his feet, each step a sharp crack that seemed too loud in the silence. The pathway stretched ahead, lined with grass so caked in dust it had surrendered any memory of green. The only light came from dim LEDs perched atop the fence posts, each one swarmed by insects whose buzzing formed a low, electric hum that pressed against the air like a living thing.

Everything he'd checked so far was clean. Empty trails that led him in circles, dead ends, nothing. But maybe this one would finally get him there. No, it had to. If this didn't work...

He reached into his pocket for a cigarette and found nothing but lint. He'd left them in the car. He turned, and the path stretched behind him, impossibly long, the distant gate a speck swallowed by darkness. No way he was walking back. Not after coming this far.

He closed his eyes, let the gravel's sharp edges press into his soles through his shoes. Then he turned and kept walking.

The Hoffman commercials had owned the plot and the hangers. They'd been about to go public when they bought the lot to store their stock, but one article had been enough to crater the company. Trueman Industrial picked up the pieces, found no use for the hangers, and started renting them out to anyone willing to pay. A clever way to squeeze money from a liability. Some of the hangers had been repurposed into workshops, others into makeshift homes. Rumor had it that a few had been converted into something far less legal, but Director Miller wasn't paid to follow rumors. He was paid to follow the truth, however twisted it became.

He walked for what felt like a long hour, his eyes tracing the rusted numbers bolted to the weathered steel walls of each hanger as he passed. Forty-seven. Thirty-one. Twelve. And then, at last, number two.

Hanger 2 stood apart from its neighbors. No lights buzzed along its fence. The grass along its sides, instead of choking on neglect, was neatly cut and still carried that deep, living green. It looked almost loved.

He approached the gate. The barbed wire hung loose, the latch undone. A faint light bled from the back of the hanger, warm and yellow. He rounded the corner and found a man hunched over a flower bed, watering a neat row of potted plants with a rusted can. He hummed as he worked, an off-key melody that wandered through a tune Miller didn't recognize.

"Lost, friend?" The man's voice came without him looking up. Warm. Almost kind. "The city's that way."

"I'm looking for Robert. Does he live here?"

The man straightened slowly, his face shifting as he turned. The warmth in his eyes flickered, replaced by something more guarded. Concern, maybe.

"My son?" he said. "He's not well today. Bad episode. I'm his father. Is there something I can help you with?"

"I'm afraid I need to speak with him directly. It's a matter of national security."

The man's brow furrowed. "National security?" He let out a short, humorless laugh. "My boy doesn't trouble anyone. He keeps to himself. His plants..."

"I'm not here about trouble. I'm here about cooperation. Against a common threat."

The man studied his face for a long moment, head tilted, weighing something Miller couldn't see. Then his expression broke into a wide, reassuring smile.

"Well, when you put it that way... Come inside, come inside. Out of this dreadful wind. I'll see if he's decent enough to receive visitors."

The hanger's interior was larger than it had seemed from outside. Industrial shelving units rose toward the ceiling, stacked high with boxes; some labeled with faded company names, others unmarked and gathering dust. The air tasted of metal dust and something faintly sweet underneath, like flowers left too long in a vase.

The man led him past the shelves, weaving through the narrow corridors of stored debris, toward a door hidden behind a stack of crates. He pulled it open, revealing concrete steps descending into dim light.

"My son's private quarters," the man said, his voice dropping. "Down here. Mind your step."

The stairs opened into a room that smelled of damp concrete and something metallic. Bare bulbs hung from cords, casting harsh shadows that seemed to shift on their own. In the center stood a metal table with two chairs. And against one wall, a man in his forties was tied to a metal pipe. An old mattress lay beneath him, stained and flattened. His eyes were wide, fixed on the newcomers with the desperate clarity of someone who had run out of hope.

Miller froze. His hand twitched toward his hip, reaching for the weapon that wasn't there; still locked in the glove compartment, per Bureau protocol for initial contact meetings. He had followed the rules. The rules had led him here.

"What in God's name is this?"

The warmth on the old man's face evaporated like mist under a midday sun. His shoulders squared, his eyes darkened, and a predator's stillness settled over him, so complete it seemed to suck the air from the room.

"I believe you've discovered my hobby, Director Miller."

The voice was no longer warm. It was cold, precise, and younger than the body it came from.

Miller backed away slowly, his eyes darting around the room, cataloging exits, potential weapons, threats. His retreat ended when his back struck the metal table, the sound ringing through the concrete space.

"Please." Robert gestured to the chair. "Sit. We have much to discuss."

Miller hesitated. His pulse hammered in his throat, loud enough that he was certain Robert could hear it. He lowered himself into the chair, his muscles coiled, ready to spring. Robert took the seat across from him, leaning forward, elbows on the table, fingers steepled beneath his chin.

"You mentioned a common threat." His voice was soft, almost intimate. "I'm intrigued."

"We've been tracking another anomaly," Miller said, forcing steadiness into his voice despite the tremor in his hands. "Someone with abilities similar to yours. We need your help to stop him."

A dry chuckle scraped out of Robert's throat. "My abilities?" He shook his head slowly. "You assume too much. And why would I help you? What could you possibly offer me?"

"Immunity. Protection. A chance to use your skills for something other than this."

"This?" Robert's eyes drifted to the man bound against the wall, then back to Miller. A smile played at the corners of his mouth. "This is art, Director. This is purpose. What you offer is a cage. Albeit a gilded one."

Miller swallowed, forced his hands flat against his thighs to stop their trembling. "His name is Malachai Cross. Bureau designation: Colossus. Six months ago he brought down a twelve-story apartment building in Detroit, by hand. Pulled the support columns out from under it like he was yanking weeds from a garden. Two hundred and eleven dead."

He paused, waiting for a reaction. Robert's face betrayed nothing.

"Los Angeles last month. He walked into a packed stadium during a concert and started tearing through the crowd. Security footage shows him throwing grown men through concrete walls. He killed eighty-three people before he got bored and left. We've tried everything. Task forces, tactical teams, military intervention. He swats them away like flies."

Robert tapped a finger against his steepled hands, once, twice. "And you believe I can stop this... Colossus."

"We have reason to believe your abilities are complementary. Where he is brute force, you are-"

"Refined." Robert smiled. "Yes, I gathered."

"We need you, Robert. The Bureau is prepared to offer you a full pardon. Immunity for all past-" Miller's eyes flicked involuntarily to the man bound against the wall, "-activities. A protected facility. Resources. Anything you need."

"Anything I need." Robert repeated the words slowly, tasting them. "And what if what I need is this?" He gestured around the room with open palms. "My garden. My privacy. My... hobbies."

"You could have all of that and more. Clean. Legal. You'd be a hero."

Robert's laugh was soft, almost pitying. "A hero. Director, do I strike you as the heroic type?"

Miller pressed on, the words coming faster now. "We could set you up somewhere better than this. A real lab. Subjects of your choosing; criminals on death row. You could continue your work without looking over your shoulder."

"For a man who walked in here with Bureau protocols and a missing sidearm, you're making rather large promises." Robert tilted his head, his eyes glinting. "Do you have the authority to offer all of this, Director? Or are you gambling that you'll figure out the details later?"

Miller's jaw tightened. "I have authorization to negotiate terms."

"Negotiate." Robert leaned back, folding his arms. "Then negotiate. Convince me."

"You'd have full autonomy. A dedicated budget. I can get you-"

"A budget." Robert's smile widened. "How generous. But tell me, Director, what use do I have for money? I live in a hanger. My needs are simple. My pleasures are... homegrown." He glanced at the bound man, who had stopped struggling and now hung limp against the pipe, his breathing shallow.

Miller followed his gaze and felt his stomach turn. He forced himself to look back at Robert. "What do you want, then?"

"I want you to keep talking."

"Excuse me?"

"Keep talking. I find your desperation fascinating. You're a man who has spent his entire life in control. You carry a badge and a gun and the full weight of the federal government behind you. You walk into rooms and people straighten their spines because you represent something they fear." Robert's voice dropped, became almost tender. "But right now, sitting across from me, you are afraid. I can smell it on you. That faint sour note beneath your cologne. The way your pulse flutters in your throat. You're terrified, and you're trying to hide it, and it's the most honest thing about you."

Miller's mouth went dry. He wanted to look away from those eyes, but found he couldn't.

"You came here because you have no other options. Your Colossus has you beaten. Your Bureau is desperate. And so they sent you; their best negotiator, their most level-headed Director, to bargain with a monster because the alternative is watching your cities fall one by one." Robert's smile didn't reach his eyes. "I'm touched by the compliment, truly. But you haven't answered my question."

"Which question?"

"What makes you think I want to be saved?"

The words hung in the air between them. Miller's mind raced, grasping for an angle, a pressure point, anything. "You're intelligent. Educated. You could have been anything; a surgeon, a scientist, a-"

"A gardener?" Robert offered.

"Is that what you call it?"

"I call it creation. You call it destruction. We're both right, and we're both wrong. But I am the only one here who is at peace with that truth."

"Peace." Miller's voice cracked. "You have a man chained to a pipe in your basement. You tortured him. For how long? Days? Weeks? And you call that peace?"

Robert's expression flickered; not anger, but something like mild disappointment. "I see you've confused peace with comfort. They are not the same thing, Director. I am at peace with what I am. You, Director, are deeply uncomfortable with what you are. That is the difference between us."

"I know exactly what I am."

"Do you?" Robert leaned forward, his eyes locking onto Miller's. "Then tell me. Why did you come alone? Why did you leave your weapon in the car? You knew what I was capable of. You had read the files. And yet you walked through my gate without backup, without a wire, without so much as a knife in your boot. Was it protocol? Or was it something else?"

Miller said nothing.

"I'll tell you what it was. You came alone because you wanted this to be clean. A quiet negotiation. A handshake. A deal struck in the dark where no one has to acknowledge what either of you really are. You don't want to be the man who bargains with killers. So you came alone, so that if this failed-" Robert spread his hands, "-no one would have to know you were here."

"That's not true."

"Isn't it?" Robert's voice was silk wrapped around a blade. "Then tell me, Director. If this is a sanctioned operation, where are the witnesses? Where is the paper trail? Where is the backup team waiting at the gate?"

Miller's throat tightened. The answer sat in his chest like a stone.

"There's no one within a mile of this hanger, is there?" Robert whispered. "You came alone because you were ashamed. Ashamed that you need me. Ashamed that the great Federal Bureau of Investigation has to crawl to a man like me and beg. You told yourself it was protocol. But we both know the truth."

The silence stretched. Miller could hear his own heartbeat, the drip of something in the corner, the shallow breathing of the man on the mattress.

"What if I said yes?" Robert asked suddenly.

Miller blinked. "What?"

"What if I said I would help you? I'd leave this place. I'd hunt your Colossus. I'd bring him down, break him however you need me to. What would you do then, Director?"

A spark of hope flickered in Miller's chest, despite everything. "I'd- we'd hold up our end. Full immunity. Protection. Whatever resources you-"

"And then what?" Robert cut him off. "After Colossus is dealt with. After the cameras stop rolling and the press conferences end. What happens to me then?"

"You'd be under Bureau protection. A secure facility. You'd have-"

"A cage." Robert's voice hardened. "A comfortable cage, but a cage nonetheless. And eventually, when the public forgets about the monster who saved them, someone in your Bureau would decide I'm too dangerous to keep alive. A bullet in the back of the head. A staged accident. And the files would be sealed for fifty years."

"We wouldn't-"

"You would." Robert's certainty was absolute. "It's what you do. It's what any system does when it's finished with a tool it can't control. I've studied your Bureau, Director. I know its habits. I know its secrets. I know that three years ago, you quietly terminated a project called Chimera because the asset became too unpredictable. I know you have a kill list with twenty-seven names on it, all former assets who outlived their usefulness."

Miller's face went pale. That information was classified above his clearance. He only knew about it through rumors.

"You see, Director, you came here thinking you were the hunter. But you've walked into my parlor, sat at my table, and now you're trying to convince me to trade a life I control for a life you control. And you haven't offered me a single thing I can't already take for myself."

The words landed like hammer blows. Miller's carefully rehearsed arguments crumbled one by one. He had nothing left. No leverage. No angle. No escape.

But as the despair settled in, something else began to surface. A strange, reckless calm. If he was going to die here; and more and more, that seemed the likely outcome, then he would die like he had lived. On his feet. Fighting.

"You're right," Miller said quietly.

Robert raised an eyebrow.

"I came here alone because I was ashamed. I left my gun in the car because I didn't want to be the kind of man who points a weapon at someone he's asking for help. I had all these scenarios mapped out; what I'd say, how you'd react, what I'd offer. I thought if I was smart enough, careful enough, I could control this."

He looked up, meeting Robert's eyes directly for the first time.

"But I was wrong. You're not someone who can be controlled. You're not someone who can be bargained with. You're not even someone who can be reasoned with." He pushed his chair back, the legs screeching against concrete. The sound was harsh, final. "You're a monster. And I can't reason with a monster."

For a long moment, Robert simply looked at him. Then a slow smile spread across his face;  not the practiced, reassuring smile from the garden, but something genuine. Something delighted.

"And I can't abide a fool."

The movement was too fast for Miller to track. One moment Robert was seated, the other he was around the table, and Miller's head snapped back as Robert's fist connected with his jaw. The world tilting sideways, and then the concrete floor rushed up to meet him. Consciousness slipped away like water through his fingers, and he was gone before he hit the ground.

---

Miller woke to the taste of blood and the throb of a skull being slowly crushed. The rope bit into his neck, the rough metal of the pipe digging into his spine. He tried to move, to shift the pressure, and the rope tightened.

"It's no use." The whisper came from beside him, hoarse and worn thin. "I've tried."

Miller blinked through the haze of pain, forcing his eyes to focus. The room swam into view. The metal table. The bare bulbs. And Robert, seated in the same chair, watching him with an unnerving stillness. Head cocked. Eyes fixed on Miller with the patient attention of a man studying a specimen under glass.

The cold air raised goosebumps on his arms. He looked down at himself; white undershirt, white underwear. Everything else was gone. His jacket, his shirt, his shoes. Stripped clean.

His eyes found them across the room. A metal clothing rack stood against the far wall, his suit and coat hanging neatly from plastic hangers, his shoes placed side by side beneath them. Beside them hung a second set of clothes.

The stare held. Seconds bled into a minute. Neither of them spoke. Miller could feel the weight of Robert's gaze pressing against him, reading him.

Then Robert rose, unhurried, and walked to the stairs. He didn't look back. The heavy door groaned shut behind him, and the lock clicked into place.

"Where does he go?" Miller's voice came out as a croak.

"Wherever he wants." The other man let out a breath that might have been a laugh. "He comes and goes. Sometimes for hours. Sometimes for half a day."

Miller twisted his head, scanning the shadows. Nothing moved. Just the slow drip of water somewhere in the dark.

"How long was I out?"

"A few hours. Maybe more." A pause. "He said he was going to water the plants."

Miller tested the ropes. They held firm, expertly tied. The pipe was anchored deep in concrete. He let his head fall back against the metal and felt the pain flare through his jaw.

"I'm Greg," the man said after a long silence.

"Miller."

"I know." The sound that escaped Greg was hollow, broken. "He told me you were coming. Said he'd been expecting a visit from the Bureau for a while."

Miller turned his head, studying him properly. Late fifties, silver hair matted with sweat. He was dressed the same as Miller; white undershirt, white underwear. The fabric was stained and grey from weeks of wear. Deep bruising ringed his wrists where the rope had sawed at him day after day, and his ribs pressed visible against the thin cotton. But his eyes, though sunken and shadowed, still held something. A stubborn ember.

"How long have you been here?"

"Three weeks." The number hung in the air between them. "Three weeks tomorrow."

"Three weeks of this?"

Greg nodded, the motion straining against the rope. "He told me it would be one month. One month in this basement, and then I walk out. Free to go."

Miller stared at the bare bulb, his mind turning. One month. Greg was three weeks in. One week left.

"What happened?" Miller asked. "How did you end up down here?"

Greg was silent for a long moment. When he spoke, his voice came out flat and distant, as if he was reading from a file that belonged to someone else.

"I had a house in Brighton. Big house. Too big, my wife always said." He paused for a while. "Three men broke in. They weren't normal criminals. One of them bent the steel doorframe like it was paper. Another moved faster than I could follow. The third could light things on fire just by staring at them."

Miller felt his pulse quicken. More of them. A whole crew with abilities.

"They tied us up. My wife Elena, my daughters, Sarah and Chloe." He paused a while. "They couldn't open the safe in the bedroom, so they made calls. Brought in someone who could."

"Robert."

"He showed up late." Greg's jaw tightened. "He talked to the others for a minute. Then he came over, grabbed me by the collar, and dragged me to the room where my family was."

The bare bulb buzzed overhead. Miller didn't dare breathe.

"He untied Elena. Told her she could go free if she opened the safe." Greg's voice cracked. "She didn't know the combination. I was the only one who did. But he didn't even let me speak."

Miller's hands curled into fists against the rope.

"He killed them. One by one. Made sure I could see everything." Greg's voice dropped to a whisper. "Then he went back to the bedroom, opened the safe himself, and walked out with the others. Ten minutes later, while I was still on the floor holding Elena's hand, he came back. Said I was too interesting to leave behind."

The silence pressed in from all sides. Even the drip of water seemed to hold its breath.

"I'm sorry," Miller said. The words felt useless.

"Save your sorry. Save your strength. You'll need it."

The hours passed in near darkness. The bare bulbs never dimmed, never flickered. Miller drifted in and out of consciousness, the rope grinding against his throat, his shoulders ablaze from holding his weight. Greg had gone quiet, his chin pressed to his chest, retreating into a place Miller couldn't follow.

He tried to think. Tried to plan. Every path led to a dead end. No weapon. No backup. No way out. Robert was faster than he could see, had a crew of powered criminals at his disposal, and spent his leisure time torturing people in a basement. Greg said one month and you walk free. But free to what? A world where your family was dead and your house was a crime scene? Free to be a ghost in a body that wouldn't stop remembering?

Or free to die in a ditch somewhere, the promise of release was just another part of the experiment.

The lock clicked.

Miller's eyes snapped open. 

The heavy door groaned open, and light spilled down the stairs; pale, grey, the unmistakable color of morning.

Footsteps on concrete. Slow. Unhurried. The same measured rhythm as before.

Robert appeared at the top of the stairs, backing through the doorway. He was dragging something heavy. A bagged head came into view first, lolling against Robert's chest. Then the torso, limp and unresisting. Then the legs, bumping down each riser in loose, rubbery arcs. Robert had his hands locked under the man's armpits, hauling him backward down the steps one at a time.

The man was unconscious. His hands were bound at the wrists with zip ties. His ankles too, lashed together with a second tie. He hung limp as a sack of grain, his head lolling with each jolt of the descent.

Robert grunted with every step. His shoulders strained, his back bent, and he paused twice to adjust his grip, letting the man's weight settle before continuing. The heels of the man's shoes thumped against each concrete riser in a dull, rhythmic beat.

Miller watched the performance with a cold, creeping clarity. He knew what Robert was. He had felt it. The speed. The precision. The strength behind that single blow that had put him on the floor. This display; this old man struggling with dead weight, was theater. Every labored breath, every feigned strain. Robert could have carried the man down one-handed without breaking rhythm. But he wanted Miller to see the act. Wanted him to sit through the performance and wonder which parts were real and which parts were for his benefit.

Robert reached the bottom and let the man drop. The body hit the concrete with a thud. Robert straightened slowly, rolled his shoulders, and wiped a hand across his brow; the picture of a man catching his breath after honest work.

He crossed to the table, pulled out the chair, and sat, folding his hands before him.

"Good morning, Director. I trust you've had time to reflect."

Miller said nothing.

Robert's gaze drifted to Greg, then settled back on Miller. A small smile touched the corners of his mouth. "I'm glad you two had a chance to talk. Greg tells his story well. I've heard it several times now. It improves with each telling."

He leaned back, hooked a thumb toward the man lying on the floor. "I was at the market. Buying soil. This gentleman saw the flowers I was carrying and felt compelled to share his opinion. Called them an inferior species. Poorly hybridized. Asked if I'd grown them from a discount seed packet."

Robert's smile sharpened.

"I was going to let it go. Truly. But then he spat on the fern."

He rose from the chair and crossed to the man on the floor. Gripped him by the hair and dragged him across the concrete until he was positioned directly in front of Miller, then hauled him upright onto his knees.

"I've decided to continue our discussion," Robert said, stepping behind the man and placing his hands on either side of his head. "But first, a demonstration. Theory is best understood when paired with practice."

He pulled the bag off the man's head in a single, sharp motion. The man gasped, waking, dragging in air like a drowning man breaking the surface. His eyes flew open, wild and uncomprehending, and then the terror caught up with him and he began to babble. "-sorry, I'm sorry, I didn't mean it, please, I have a wife, I have kids, please, I'll do anything-"

Robert placed a finger to his own lips. The man kept begging.

Robert sighed, reached down, and gripped the man's jaw, squeezing until the words cut off into a whimper. He held it there a moment, then released it, letting the man's head drop. The man sagged forward, sobbing into the concrete.

Robert watched him for a moment. Then he said, very quietly, "You have a daughter. Eight years old. Her name is Lily."

The man's sobbing stopped.

"She has a stuffed rabbit on her bed," Robert continued, his voice carrying the same casual warmth he had used in the garden. "Pink. One of the ears is torn. She sleeps with it every night. You kissed her goodbye this morning before you left for work."

The man's face had gone the color of ash.

"I know where you live. I know where she goes to school. I know the route your wife takes to pick her up. I know every lock on every door of your house, and I can tell you, with some authority, that none of them would slow me down."

He let that settle.

"Now. Here is the only choice you have left." He produced a small pruning shear from his pocket, the blades curved and sharp. "I'm going to count to three. On three, I want you to look at me. I want you to look me in the eyes, and I want you to say 'thank you.' If you do that, I'll let you keep both hands."

The man stared at the shears, his breath coming in shallow, hitching gasps.

"One."

The man began to cry.

"Two."

He tried to speak, but nothing came out.

"Three."

The man's eyes found Robert's. His mouth opened, closed, opened again. A sound forced its way out, small and broken. "Th-thank you."

Robert smiled, warm and bright. He slipped the shears back into his pocket. "Good man. See? That wasn't so hard."

He clapped the man on the shoulder. Then Robert produced a lighter from his pocket and clicked it to life. The flame was small, blue at the base and gold at the tip, barely larger than a candle. He held it up between them, letting Miller watch the heat shimmer the air.

Then he pressed it to the man's forearm.

The skin blanched white, then blistered, then blackened. The man screamed, a sound that started deep in his chest and climbed until it broke apart in his throat. Robert held the flame steady for a three-count, then lifted it and examined his work. A perfect circle, the size of a coin.

He moved to a fresh patch of skin and pressed again.

The second circle overlapped the first by a hair. He adjusted, pressed a third. The man's arm twitched and jumped, but Robert's hand was stone-still, his focus absolute. He worked like a man signing his name; each burn deliberate, spaced evenly, building a pattern that only he could see. Circle after circle crawled up the forearm, over the elbow, across the bicep. The air filled with the smell of cooked meat and the sound of the man's voice fraying into a raw, continuous keen.

When both arms were covered from wrist to shoulder in overlapping burn rings, Robert clicked off the torch and set it aside. He examined his work, tilting his head, running a thumb lightly over one of the welts. The man whimpered and tried to pull away, but there was nowhere to go.

Robert reached for his left hand.

He took the index finger between his thumb and forefinger, found the first knuckle, and squeezed. The man's eyes went wide. Robert began to lecture in the same tone a professor might use to explain a theorem.

"The proximal interphalangeal joint," he said, turning the finger gently, feeling the structure beneath the skin. "A hinge joint. Remarkably simple. In most people, it can withstand roughly thirty pounds of pressure before the ligaments begin to fail."

He applied pressure. The man gasped.

"Thirty-one." A pop, soft as a twig snapping in wet grass. The man screamed. "Thirty-two." Another pop. "The fascinating thing about fingers is how little force is actually required to break them. The body makes us believe we are durable. But we are not."

He released the finger. It hung at an angle it was not meant to hang.

He moved to the middle finger.

"The Metacarpophalangeal joint." Pressure. A crack. The man's scream had lost its edges, blurring into a single sustained note. "When the bone snaps, the tendon often remains intact. This allows for a rather interesting phenomenon." He bent the broken finger backward. The tip touched the back of the man's hand. "The finger can be made to point in directions the mind knows are impossible. The confusion between what the eye sees and what the nerves report is, in its own way, a second injury."

He moved to the ring finger. Then the pinky. Then the thumb.

Each one received the same careful attention. The same lecture. The same unhurried pressure. The man had stopped screaming by the time Robert reached the right hand; not because it hurt less, but because his voice had given out. What came out of his throat now was a dry, clicking rasp, air forced past vocal cords locked in a permanent spasm.

Robert worked through the right hand with the same methodical patience. Thumb. Index. Middle. Ring. Pinky. Ten fingers in total. Ten separate breaks. Ten small lectures delivered in that calm, carrying voice.

When he was finished, he sat back and let the man's hands drop into his lap. The fingers lay across his thighs like a handful of snapped twigs, bent at angles that made the eye want to look away.

The man's screams had become whimpers. The whimpers had become choked sobs that no longer sounded human.

Throughout it all, Robert held Miller's gaze. His expression was one of intense concentration; the look of an artist adding the final brushstrokes to a masterpiece. Miller's horror was the last ingredient needed to perfect the creation.

Beside him, Greg had turned his face away, his eyes squeezed shut. But the sounds didn't stop. They never stopped.

When the man was barely conscious, barely breathing, Robert retrieved a long, thin knife from the table. It caught the light as he turned it over, examining the blade with obvious satisfaction.

"You see, Director," he said, stepping closer, "the problem with your world is that you've forgotten a simple truth. Pain is the only thing that's real. It's the only thing that lasts."

He pressed the knife against the bound man's chest, directly over his heart. The man didn't even react; his eyes had rolled back, his consciousness fled to some distant place where pain couldn't follow.

Robert leaned in close, his voice dropping to a whisper. "And I am its high priest."

The blade pushed forward with agonizing slowness. The man's body convulsed once; a violent, full-body spasm, and then hung limp. The only sound was the rhythmic drip of blood onto concrete, a metronome marking the seconds.

Robert wiped the blade on the dead man's shirt, then turned his full attention to Miller. He crossed the distance slowly, savoring the distance, and stopped inches from Miller's face. His breath was warm, carrying the faint sweetness of rotting flowers.

"Now," he said, his voice soft, almost affectionate. "About that cooperation. I've reconsidered your offer."

Miller flinched as Robert reached out, but Robert only adjusted the rope around his neck, tightening it just enough to make each breath a conscious effort.

"I'll give you a week." Robert's smile could have been carved from ice. "One week to reconsider your perspective. To truly understand what it means to be powerless. Then we'll talk again."

He released the rope, stepped back, and stretched with the casual satisfaction of a man finishing a long day's work. He walked toward the stairs, his footsteps echoing through the concrete chamber.

At the top, he paused, looking back over his shoulder. "Oh, and Director? Try not to scream too much. It upsets the plants."

The heavy door groaned shut, and the lock clicked into place. The basement plunged into near darkness, lit only by the faint glow of the bare bulbs. Miller was left alone with the dead man's body, the drip of blood marking time, and the cold certainty that in seven days, whatever remained of his mind would belong to Robert.
